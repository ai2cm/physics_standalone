5 !> \defgroup GFS_gwd GFS Orographic Gravity Wave Drag
6 !! @{
7 !! Parameterization developed specifically for orographic and
8 !! convective source of gravity waves are documented separately.
9 !!
10 !! At present, global models must be run with horizontal resolutions
11 !! that cannot typically resolve atmospheric phenomena shorter than
12 !! ~10-100 km or greater for weather prediction and ~100-1000 km or
13 !! greater for climate predicition. Many atmospheric processes have
14 !! shorter horizontal scales than these "subgrid-scale" processes
15 !! interact with and affect the larger-scale atmosphere in important
16 !! ways.
17 !!
18 !! Atmospheric gravity waves are one such unresolved processes. These
19 !! waves are generated by lower atmospheric sources. e.g., flow over
20 !! irregularities at the Earth's surface such as mountains and valleys,
21 !! uneven distribution of diabatic heat sources asscociated with
22 !! convective systems, and highly dynamic atmospheric processes such
23 !! as jet streams and fronts. The dissipation of these waves produces
24 !! synoptic-scale body forces on the atmospheric flow, known as
25 !! "gravity wave drag"(GWD), which affects both short-term evolution
26 !! of weather systems and long-term climate. However, the spatial
27 !! scales of these waves (in the range of ~5-500 km horizontally) are
28 !! too short to be fully captured in models, and so GWD must be
29 !! parameterized. In addition, the role of GWD in driving the global
30 !! middle atmosphere circulation and thus global mean wind/temperature
31 !! structures is well established. Thus, GWD parametrizations are now
32 !! critical components of virtually all large-scale atmospheric models.
33 !! GFS physics includes parameterizations of gravity waves from two
34 !! important sources: mountains and convection.
35 !!
36 !! Atmospheric flow is significantly influenced by orography creating
37 !! lift and frictional forces. The representation of orography and its
38 !! influence in numerical weather prediction models are necessarily
39 !! divided into the resolvable scales of motion and treated by
40 !! primitive equations, the remaining sub-grid scales to be treated by
41 !! parameterization. In terms of large scale NWP models, mountain
42 !! blocking of wind flow around sub-grid scale orograph is a process
43 !! that retards motion at various model vertical levels near or in the
44 !! boundary layer. Flow around the mountain encounters larger
45 !! frictional forces by being in contact with the mountain surfaces
46 !! for longer time as well as the interaction of the atmospheric
47 !! environment with vortex shedding which occurs in numerous
48 !! observations. Lott and Miller (1997) \cite lott_and_miller_1997,
49 !! incorporated the dividing streamline and mountain blocking in
50 !! conjunction with sub-grid scale vertically propagating gravity wave
51 !! parameterization in the context of NWP. The dividing streamline is
52 !! seen as a source of gravity waves to the atmosphere above and
53 !! nonlinear subgrid low-level mountain drag effect below.
54 !!
55 !! In a review paper on gravity waves in the middle atmosphere, Fritts
56 !! (1984) \cite fritts_1984 showed that a large portion of observed
57 !! gravity wave momentum flux has higher frequencies than those of
58 !! stationary mountain waves. This phenomenon was explained by cumulus
59 !! convection, which is an additional source of tropospheric gravity
60 !! waves, and is particularly important in summertime. When the surface
61 !! wind and stability are weak, the magnitude of the surface drag and
62 !! the resultant influence of orographically-induced gravity wave drag
63 !! on the large-scale flow are relatively small compared with those in
64 !! wintertime (Palmer et al. 1986 \cite palmer_et_al_1986). In this
65 !! situation, the relative importance of cumulus convection as a source
66 !! of gravity waves is larger. In addition, in the tropical regions
67 !! where persistent convection exists, deep cumulus clouds impinging on
68 !! the stable stratosphere can generate gravity waves that influence
69 !! the large-scale flow.
70 !!
71 !> \section outlines GWD parameterization in GFS
72 !! - Gravity-wave drag is simulated as described by Alpert et al.
73 !! (1988) \cite alpert_et_al_1988. The parameterization includes
74 !! determination of the momentum flux due to gravity waves at the
75 !! surface, as well as upper levels. The surface stress is a nonlinear
76 !! function of the surface wind speed and the local Froude number,
77 !! following Pierrehumbert (1987) \cite pierrehumbert_1987. Vertical
78 !! variations in the momentum flux occur when the local Richardson
79 !! number is less than 0.25 (the stress vanishes), or when wave
80 !! breaking occurs (local Froude number becomes critical); in the
81 !! latter case, the momentum flux is reduced according to the
82 !! Lindzen(1981) \cite lindzen_1981 wave saturation hypothesis.
83 !! Modifications are made to avoid instability when the critical layer
84 !! is near the surface, since the time scale for gravity-wave drag is
85 !! shorter than the model time step.
86 !!
87 !! - The treatment of the GWD in the lower troposphere is enhanced
88 !! according to Kim and Arakawa (1995) \cite kim_and_arakawa_1995 .
89 !! Orographic Std Dev (HPRIME), Convexity(OC), Asymmetry (OA4) and Lx
90 !! (CLX4) are input topographic statistics needed (see Appendix in Kim
91 !! and Arakawa (1995) \cite kim_and_arakawa_1995) .
92 !!
93 !! - Mountain blocking influences are incorporated following the Lott
94 !! and Miller (1997) \cite lott_and_miller_1997 parameterization with
95 !! minor changes, including their dividing streamline concept. The
96 !! model subgrid scale orography is represented by four parameters,
97 !! after Baines and Palmer (1990) \cite baines_and_palmer_1990, the
98 !! standard deviation (HPRIME), the anisotropy (GAMMA), the slope
99 !! (SIGMA) and the geographical orientation of the orography (THETA).
100 !! These are calculated off-line as a function of model resolution in
101 !! the fortran code ml01rg2.f, with script mlb2.sh (see Appendix:
102 !! Specification of subgrid-scale orography in Lott and Miller (1997)
103 !! \cite lott_and_miller_1997).
104 !!
105 !! - The orographic GWD parameterizations automatically scales
106 !! with model resolution. For example, the T574L64 version of GFS uses
107 !! four times stronger mountain blocking and one half the strength of
108 !! gravity wave drag than the T383L64 version.
109 !!

118 !! This subroutine includes orographic gravity wave drag and mountain
119 !! blocking.
120 !!
121 !> The time tendencies of zonal and meridional wind are altered to
122 !! include the effect of mountain induced gravity wave drag from
123 !! subgrid scale orography including convective breaking, shear
124 !! breaking and the presence of critical levels.
125 !!
126 !! The NWP model gravity wave drag (GWD) scheme in the GFS has two
127 !! main components: how the surface stress is computed, and then how
128 !! that stress is distributed over a vertical column where it may
129 !! interact with the models momentum. Each of these depends on the
130 !! large scale environmental atmospheric state and assumptions about
131 !! the sub-grid scale processes. In Alpert GWD (1987) based on linear,
132 !! two-dimensional non-rotating, stably stratified flow over a mountain ridge,
133 !! sub-grid scale gravity wave motions are assumed which propagate away
134 !! from the mountain. Described in Alpert (1987), the flux measured over
135 !! a "low level" vertically averaged layer, in the atmosphere defines a base
136 !! level flux. "Low level" was taken to be the first 1/3 of the troposphere
137 !! in the 1987 implementation. This choice was meant to encompass a thick
138 !! low layer for vertical averages of the environmental (large scale) flow
139 !! quantities. The vertical momentum flux or gravity wave stress in a
140 !! grid box due to a single mountain is given as in Pierrehumbert, (1987) (PH):
141 !!
142 !! \f$ \tau = \frac {\rho \: U^{3}\: G(F_{r})} {\Delta X \; N } \f$
143 !!
144 !! emetic \f$ \Delta X \f$ is a grid increment, N is the Brunt Viasala frequency
145 !!
146 !!
147 !! \f$ N(\sigma) = \frac{-g \: \sigma \:
148 !! \frac{\partial\Theta}{\partial\sigma}}{\Theta \:R \:T} \f$
149 !!
150 !! The environmental variables are calculated from a mass weighted vertical
151 !! average over a base layer. G(Fr) is a monotonically increasing
152 !! function of Froude number,
153 !!
154 !! \f$ F_{r} = \frac{N h^{'}}{U} \f$
155 !!
156 !! where U is the wind speed calculated as a mass weighted vertical average in
157 !! the base layer, and h', is the vertical displacement caused by the orography
158 !! variance. An effective mountain length for the gravity wave processes,
159 !!
160 !! \f$ l^{*} = \frac{\Delta X}{m} \f$
161 !!
162 !! where m is the number of mountains in a grid box, can then
163 !! be defined to obtain the form of the base level stress
164 !!
165 !!
166 !! \f$ \tau = \frac {\rho \: U^{3} \: G(F_{r})} {N \;l^{*}} \f$
167 !!
168 !! giving the stress induced from the surface in a model grid box.
169 !! PH gives the form for the function G(Fr) as
170 !!
171 !!
172 !! \f$ G(F_{r}) = \bar{G}\frac{F^{2}_{r}}{F^{2}_{r}\: + \:a^{2}} \f$
173 !!
174 !! Where \f$ \bar{G} \f$ is an order unity non-dimensional saturation
175 !! flux set to 1 and 'a' is a function of the mountain aspect ratio also
176 !!set to 1 in the 1987 implementation of the GFS GWD. Typical values of
177 !! U=10m/s, N=0.01 1/s, l*=100km, and a=1, gives a flux of 1 Pascal and
178 !! if this flux is made to go to zero linearly with height then the
179 !! decelerations would be about 10/m/s/day which is consistent with
180 !! observations in PH.
181 !!
182 !!
183 !! In Kim, Moorthi, Alpert's (1998, 2001) GWD currently in GFS operations,
184 !! the GWD scheme has the same physical basis as in Alpert (1987) with the addition
185 !! of enhancement factors for the amplitude, G, and mountain shape details
186 !! in G(Fr) to account for effects from the mountain blocking. A factor,
187 !! E m, is an enhancement factor on the stress in the Alpert '87 scheme.
188 !! The E ranges from no enhancement to an upper limit of 3, E=E(OA)[1-3],
189 !! and is a function of OA, the Orographic Asymmetry defined in KA (1995) as
190 !!
191 !! Orographic Asymmetry (OA) = \f$ \frac{ \bar{x} \; - \;
192 !! \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N_{b}} x_{j} \; n_{j} }{\sigma_{x}} \f$
193 !!
194 !! where Nb is the total number of bottom blocks in the mountain barrier,
195 !! \f$ \sigma_{x} \f$ is the standard deviation of the horizontal distance defined by
196 !!
197 !! \f$ \sigma_{x} = \sqrt{ \frac{\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N_{b}}
198 !! \; (x_{j} \; - \; \bar{x} )^2}{N_{x}} } \f$
199 !!
200 !!
201 !! where Nx is the number of grid intervals for the large scale domain being
202 !! considered. So the term, E(OA)m/ \f$ \Delta X \f$ in Kim's scheme represents
203 !! a multiplier on G shown in Alpert's eq (1), where m is the number of mountains
204 !! in a sub-grid scale box. Kim increased the complexity of m making it a
205 !! function of the fractional area of the sub-grid mountain and the asymmetry
206 !! and convexity statistics which are found from running a gravity wave
207 !! model for a large number of cases:
208 !!
209 !! \f$ m^{'} = C_{m} \Delta X \left[ \frac{1 \; + \;
210 !! \sum\limits_{x} L_{h} }{\Delta X} \right]^{OA+1} \f$
211 !!
212 !! Where, according to Kim, \f$ \sum \frac{L_{h}}{\Delta X} \f$ is
213 !! the fractional area covered by the subgrid-scale orography higher than
214 !! a critical height \f$ h_{c} = Fr_{c} U_{0}/N_{0} \f$ , over the
215 !! "low level" vertically averaged layer, for a grid box with the interval
216 !! \f$ \Delta X \f$. Each \f$ L_{n}\f$ is the width of a segment of
217 !! orography intersection at the critical height:
218 !!
219 !! \f$ Fr_{0} = \frac{N_{0} \; h^{'}}{U_{0}} \f$
220 !!
221 !! \f$ G^{'}(OC,Fr_{0}) = \frac{Fr_{0}^{2}}{Fr_{0}^{2} \; + \; a^{2}} \f$
222 !!
223 !! \f$ a^{2} = \frac{C_{G}}{OC} \f$
224 !!
225 !! \f$ E(OA, Fr_{0}) = (OA \; + \; 2)^{\delta} \f$ and \f$ \delta
226 !! \; = \; \frac{C_{E} \; Fr_{0}}{Fr_{c}} \f$ where \f$ Fr_{c} \f$
227 !! is as in Alpert.
228 !!
229 !!
230 !! This represents a closed scheme, somewhat empirical adjustments
231 !! to the original scheme to calculate the surface stress.
232 !!
233 !! Momentum is deposited by the sub-grid scale gravity waves break due
234 !! to the presence of convective mixing assumed to occur when the
235 !! minimum Richardson number:
236 !!
237 !! Orographic Convexity (OC) = \f$ \frac{ \sum\limits_{j=1}^{N_{x}}
238 !! \; (h_{j} \; - \; \bar{h})^4 }{N_{x} \;\sigma_{h}^4} \f$ ,
239 !! and where \f$ \sigma_{h} = \sqrt{ \frac{\sum\limits_{j=1}^{N_{x}}
240 !! \; (h_{j} \; - \; \bar{h} )^2}{N_{x}} } \f$
241 !!
242 !! This represents a closed scheme, somewhat empirical adjustments
243 !! to the original scheme to calculate the surface stress.
244 !!
245 !! Momentum is deposited by the sub-grid scale gravity waves break due
246 !! to the presence of convective mixing assumed to occur when
247 !! the minimum Richardson number:
248 !!
249 !! \f$ Ri_{m} = \frac{Ri(1 \; - \; Fr)}{(1 \; + \; \sqrt{Ri}Fr)^2} \f$
250 !!
251 !! Is less than 1/4 Or if critical layers are encountered in a layer
252 !! the the momentum flux will vanish. The critical layer is defined
253 !! when the base layer wind becomes perpendicular to the environmental
254 !! wind. Otherwise, wave breaking occurs at a level where the amplification
255 !! of the wave causes the local Froude number or similarly a truncated
256 !! (first term of the) Scorer parameter, to be reduced below a critical
257 !! value by the saturation hypothesis (Lindzen,). This is done through
258 !! eq 1 which can be written as
259 !!
260 !! \f$ \tau = \rho U N k h^{'2} \f$
261 !!
262 !! For small Froude number this is discretized in the vertical so at each
263 !! level the stress is reduced by ratio of the Froude or truncated Scorer
264 !! parameter, \f$ \frac{U^{2}}{N^{2}} = \frac{N \tau_{l-1}}{\rho U^{3} k} \f$ ,
265 !! where the stress is from the layer below beginning with that found near
266 !! the surface. The respective change in momentum is applied in
267 !! that layer building up from below.
268 !!
269 !! An amplitude factor is part of the calibration of this scheme which is
270 !! a function of the model resolution and the vertical diffusion. This
271 !! is because the vertical diffusion and the GWD account encompass
272 !! similar physical processes. Thus, one needs to run the model over
273 !! and over for various amplitude factors for GWD and vertical diffusion.
274 !!
275 !! In addition, there is also mountain blocking from lift and frictional
276 !! forces. Improved integration between how the GWD is calculated and
277 !! the mountain blocking of wind flow around sub-grid scale orography
278 !! is underway at NCEP. The GFS already has convectively forced GWD
279 !! an independent process. The next step is to test
280 !!
